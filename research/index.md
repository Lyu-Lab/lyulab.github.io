# Our research

The long-term goal of our research is to understand the molecular interactions between small molecules and their biological targets, and to turn that understanding into software for discovering novel ligands. These ligands can illuminate biological processes and help evaluate emerging therapeutic targets.

We are particularly interested in **molecular docking**, a structure-based virtual screening method that can evaluate virtual libraries of great size and diversity, followed by selection of the best-fitting molecules for synthesis and experimental testing. We leverage expansive chemical space and "make-on-demand" virtual libraries containing billions of molecules for structure-based drug design and for building chemical tools to study biology.

</div>

<div class="row">

### Developing new molecular docking algorithms

<div class="col-md-7 order-md-1">

As make-on-demand libraries grow from millions to billions to trillions of molecules, both the accuracy and the efficiency of docking must keep pace. We develop new scoring and sampling methods that improve hit rates and affinities while scaling to ultra-large libraries, and we study how library size and composition shape what docking can find.

### Testing docking on understudied targets with therapeutic potential

<div class="col-md-7 order-md-2">

We apply large-library docking prospectively against biologically and therapeutically important targets — including GPCRs, receptors, and emerging targets — to discover potent and selective ligands with novel chemotypes. Recent work has shown that even AlphaFold2-predicted structures can guide prospective ligand discovery.

</div>

### Building computational tools for new chemogenetic systems

<div class="col-md-7 order-md-1">

We are pushing the boundaries of chemogenetics by identifying new chemotypes that can selectively control engineered proteins. The aim is to overcome the limitations of existing chemogenetic ligands and enable broader, well-controlled use *in vivo* for biological studies.
